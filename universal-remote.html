How could a mobile app replace traditional remotes?

With a stereo, cable box, TV, and dvd player, you'd have to switch between four remotes. That’s crazy.

TV remotes are archaic, overcomplicated, and a TV control app would put less between devices and content.

A survey of the prospective audience

Through a Typeform survey and mechanical turk, I gathered context.

View the statistics or View the responses

The good

37% of respondents mentioned proximity or ease of use
  • I like that the remote has glow-in-the-dark buttons, which makes it easy to see in the dark. I also like the shape of it. It feels very good in my hand.
  • The remote controls all of the devices off one remote. Pressing the power button turns off the TV and the cable box together.
  • On/Off buttons, changing displays, controlling volumes…

The bad

26% of respondents mentioned frustrations with remote buttons
  • The fact that I need two remotes to properly control my devices
  • There are too many buttons that I never use, which get in the way sometimes.
    It took a while for me to remember where some of my go-to buttons were.
  • There are a lot of buttons that I never use. I don't know what they do. I would like bigger buttons.
From the survey, I knew I had to address the initial set up and intuitive, relevant, interface.

Competitors

Peel

Pros

  1. TV guide of upcoming shows
  2. Personalized TV recommendations
  3. Controls both TV and cablebox

Cons

  1. No volume control for a separate stereo box
  2. No DVD control, only TV and cablebox.
  3. No mute button

Roomie

  1. Optimizes interface based on synced remote
  2. Cable, satellite, and antenna television listings
  3. Automated actions by programming common use-cases
  1. Frustrating first time set-up, lack of documentation
  2. Requires monthly subscription for infrared devices
  3. Each additional control of a remote is $25

Logitech

  1. Lots of device compatibility, including Roku, projectors, or a PS4
  2. Easy set up
  3. Gestures actions for power users
  1. Must be to be paired with a Harmony physical device
  2. Unable to use the same controls for different devices
  3. Requires internet connection to work

Use cases & Personas

    Adrian
    The casual watcher

  1. Adrian lives in the suburbs of long island and recently got a cable-subscription for her new TV. She doesn't like how cluttered remotes can be, and just wants to watch Friends reruns every night and nothing else. She wants something simple she can use from her phone because she always loses the remote. She wants to turn on the TV and cablebox, find the show, and adjust the volume.

    2. Jim
    The power user

  2. Jim hates replacing the batteries in both his TV remote, and cablebox remote. Him and his family always have the TV on, even if just for background noise, fight over who can flip between channels. He wants a simple interface that everyone can use from their own devices.

Learning from the user tests

Link to prototype

All participants easily got through the initial set-up.
All participants had trouble with the volume and channel on separate pages.

Top findings

  1. For people who do have a cable subscription, channel and volume on same page is pertinent.
  2. For people who have more than two remotes, prompt them to take a picture in beginning, and intelligently change the UI behind it.
  3. For adding another remote, add a way to go back.
